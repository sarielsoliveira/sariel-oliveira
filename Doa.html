Doa

App and website for product donation

Designed and developed by Sariel Oliveira

Overview of the project:

DOA is a mobile app and responsive website for donating products, from clothing and furniture to electronics. Its goal is to encourage reuse, contribute to environmental care, and promote sustainability.

Project Duration:

February 2025 to April 2025

My Role:

UI/UX Designer, from ideation to final delivery.

Responsabilities:

  • Conducting user interviews.
  • Creating digital and paper wireframes.
  • Developing low- and high-fidelity prototypes.
  • Performing usability testing.
  • Applying accessibility standards.
  • Iterating on designs.
  • Outlining the information architecture.
  • Implementing responsive design.

Paper Wireframes:

I sketched out primary and secondary page wireframes.
Conceptualizing the entire app on paper helped streamline the transition to digital—both low- and high-fidelity prototypes, making the design process faster and more effective.

This approach allowed me to plan various layouts, structures, and user flows with greater clarity and precision.

Low-Fidelity Prototype:

After sketching a preliminary layout on paper, I moved on to creating the initial digital designs for the Doa app.

These early designs focused on guiding users through the core functions on the home screen, clearly highlighting key features such as posting a product, favorites, user profile, and search—all represented through intuitive icons.

Primary User Flow:

Acquire products via Favorites and Search.
Post a new product directly from the home screen.

Secondary User Flow:

Edit address, payment method, profile, log out, delete account, view purchase history...

These flows were designed to gather deeper usability feedback and improve overall user experience.

Usability Testing:

Type of Study: Unmoderated.
Location: Spain and Brazil.
Participants: 5.
Duration: 15–20 minutes.

Findings:

Based on the usability testing results, I applied several design changes to improve the overall user experience:

Repositioned the search bar to the top of the screen for greater visibility.

Removed the “search by category” option to streamline navigation.

Eliminated the introductory description of Doa from the homepage to reduce visual clutter and focus users on core actions.

High-Fidelity Prototype:

For this project, an asset library was developed in Figma to ensure a consistent visual identity across the entire application.

Each icon, image, component, and layout was designed to ensure an intuitive and engaging user experience.

The use of reusable components optimized the design process, enabling quick and efficient adjustments. This methodology led to greater visual consistency and allowed the product to evolve in an agile and structured way.

The high-fidelity prototype followed the same user flow as the low-fidelity version.

Primary User Flow:

Users can acquire products either through the Favorites section or via the Search function, allowing for quick access to saved items or easy discovery of new ones.

The option to publish a product is available directly from the home screen, streamlining the process and encouraging active user participation.

Secondary User Flow:

The app also supports secondary actions such as editing the delivery address, managing payment methods, updating the user profile, logging out, account deletion, and viewing a history of acquired products— ensuring users maintain full control over their experience.

Responsive Designs:

The designs were adapted for different screen sizes, covering both mobile and desktop devices.
I optimized each layout to meet the specific needs of users based on their device and screen resolution, ensuring a seamless and responsive experience across platforms.

Conclusions:

Impact:

The application fosters sustainability by supporting both the environment and the community. It encourages conscious consumption and empowers users to participate in a circular economy— helping one another through mindful sharing.

A quote from peer feedback beautifully captures this spirit:
“I loved the idea. So often I want to get rid of clothes I no longer wear, but I’d much rather know they’re going to someone who will actually use them.”

What I Learned:

A key takeaway from this project was the value of planning most of the design—including interactions and color choices—by sketching on paper first. This approach gave me greater creative freedom and allowed me to explore ideas more fluidly. As a result, the transition to both low- and high-fidelity digital layouts became quicker and more structured.
